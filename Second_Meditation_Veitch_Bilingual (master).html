Meditatio II – De natura mentis humanae : quod ipsa sit notior quàm corpus Meditation II – Of the Nature of the Human Mind; and that it is More Easily Known Than the Body.
1. In tantas dubitationes hesternâ meditatione conjectus sum, ut nequeam ampliùs earum oblivisci, 1. The Meditation of yesterday has filled my mind with so many doubts, that it is no longer in my power to forget them.
nec videam tamen quâ ratione solvendae sint; sed, tanquam in profundum gurgitem ex improviso delapsus, ita turbatus sum, ut nec possim in imo pedem figere, nec enatare ad summum. Nor do I see, meanwhile, any principle on which they can be resolved; and, just as if I had fallen all of a sudden into very deep water, I am so greatly disconcerted as to be unable either to plant my feet firmly on the bottom or sustain myself by swimming on the surface.
Enitar tamen & tentabo rursus eandem viam quam heri fueram ingressus, removendo scilicet illud omne quod vel minimum dubitationis admittit, nihilo secius quàm si omnino falsum esse comperissem; pergamque porro donec aliquid certi, vel, si nihil aliud, saltem hoc ipsum pro certo, nihil esse certi, cognoscam. I will, nevertheless, make an effort, and try anew the same path on which I had entered yesterday, that is, proceed by casting aside all that admits of the slightest doubt, not less than if I had discovered it to be absolutely false; and I will continue always in this track until I shall find something that is certain, or at least, if I can do nothing more, until I shall know with certainty that there is nothing certain.
Nihil nisi punctum petebat Archimedes, quod esset firmum & immobile, ut integram terram loco dimoveret; magna quoque speranda sunt, si vel minimum quid invenero quod certum sit & inconcussum. Archimedes, that he might transport the entire globe from the place it occupied to another, demanded only a point that was firm and immovable; so, also, I shall be entitled to entertain the highest expectations, if I am fortunate enough to discover only one thing that is certain and indubitable.
2. Suppono igitur omnia quae video falsa esse; credo nihil unquam extitisse eorum quae mendax memoria repraesentat; nullos plane habeo sensus; corpus, figura, extensio, motus, locusque sunt chimerae. 2. I suppose, accordingly, that all the things which I see are false (fictitious); I believe that none of those objects which my fallacious memory represents ever existed; I suppose that I possess no senses; I believe that body, figure, extension, motion, and place are merely fictions of my mind.
Quid igitur erit verum? What is there, then, that can be esteemed true?
Fortassis hoc unum, nihil esse certi. Perhaps this only, that there is absolutely nothing certain.
3. Sed unde scio nihil esse diversum ab iis omnibus quae jam jam recensui, de quo ne minima quidem occasio sit dubitandi? 3. But how do I know that there is not something different altogether from the objects I have now enumerated, of which it is impossible to entertain the slightest doubt?
Nunquid est aliquis Deus, vel quocunque nomine illum vocem, qui mihi has ipsas cogitationes immittit? Is there not a God, or some being, by whatever name I may designate him, who causes these thoughts to arise in my mind?
Quare verò hoc putem, cùm forsan ipsemet illarum author esse possim? But why suppose such a being, for it may be I myself am capable of producing them?
Nunquid ergo saltem ego aliquid sum? Am I, then, at least not something?
Sed jam negavi me habere ullos sensus, & ullum corpus. But I before denied that I possessed senses or a body;
Haereo tamen; nam quid inde? I hesitate, however, for what follows from that?
Sumne ita corpori sensibusque alligatus, ut sine illis esse non possim? Am I so dependent on the body and the senses that without these I cannot exist?
Sed mihi persuasi nihil plane esse in mundo, nullum coelum, nullam terram, nullas mentes, nulla corpora; nonne igitur etiam me non esse? But I had the persuasion that there was absolutely nothing in the world, that there was no sky and no earth, neither minds nor bodies; was I not, therefore, at the same time, persuaded that I did not exist?
Imo certe ego eram, si quid mihi persuasi. Far from it; I assuredly existed, since I was persuaded.
Sed est deceptor nescio quis, summe potens, summe callidus, qui de industriâ me semper fallit. But there is I know not what being, who is possessed at once of the highest power and the deepest cunning, who is constantly employing all his ingenuity in deceiving me.
Haud dubie igitur ego etiam sum, si me fallit; & fallat quantum potest, nunquam tamen efficiet, ut nihil sim quamdiu me aliquid esse cogitabo. Doubtless, then, I exist, since I am deceived; and, let him deceive me as he may, he can never bring it about that I am nothing, so long as I shall be conscious that I am something.
Adeo ut, omnibus satis superque pensitatis, denique statuendum sit hoc pronuntiatum, Ego sum, ego existo, quoties a me profertur, vel mente concipitur, necessario esse verum. So that it must, in fine, be maintained, all things being maturely and carefully considered, that this proposition I am, I exist, is necessarily true each time it is expressed by me, or conceived in my mind.
4. Nondum verò satis intelligo, quisnam sim ego ille, qui jam necessario sum; deincepsque cavendum est ne forte quid aliud imprudenter assumam in locum meî, sicque aberrem etiam in eâ cognitione, quam omnium certissimam evidentissimamque esse contendo. 4. But I do not yet know with sufficient clearness what I am, though assured that I am; and hence, in the next place, I must take care, lest perchance I inconsiderately substitute some other object in room of what is properly myself, and thus wander from truth, even in that knowledge (cognition) which I hold to be of all others the most certain and evident.
Quare jam denuo meditabor quidnam me olim esse crediderim, priusquam in has cogitationes incidissem; ex quo deinde subducam quidquid allatis rationibus vel minimum potuit infirmari, ut ita tandem praecise remaneat illud tantùm quod certum est & inconcussum. For this reason, I will now consider anew what I formerly believed myself to be, before I entered on the present train of thought; and of my previous opinion I will retrench all that can in the least be invalidated by the grounds of doubt I have adduced, in order that there may at length remain nothing but what is certain and indubitable.
5. Quidnam igitur antehac me esse putavi? 5. What then did I formerly think I was?
Hominem scilicet. Undoubtedly I judged that I was a man.
Sed quid est homo? But what is a man?
Dicamne animal rationale? Shall I say a rational animal?
Non, quia postea quaerendum foret quidnam animal sit, & quid rationale, atque ita ex unâ quaestione in plures difficilioresque delaberer; nec jam mihi tantum otii est, ut illo velim inter istiusmodi subtilitates abuti. Assuredly not; for it would be necessary forthwith to inquire into what is meant by animal, and what by rational, and thus, from a single question, I should insensibly glide into others, and these more difficult than the first; nor do I now possess enough of leisure to warrant me in wasting my time amid subtleties of this sort.
Sed hîc potius attendam, quid sponte & naturâ duce cogitationi meae antehac occurrebat, quoties quid essem considerabam. I prefer here to attend to the thoughts that sprung up of themselves in my mind, and were inspired by my own nature alone, when I applied myself to the consideration of what I was.
Nempe occurrebat primo, me habere vultum, manus, brachia, totamque hanc membrorum machinam, qualis etiam in cadavere cernitur, & quam corporis nomine designabam. In the first place, then, I thought that I possessed a countenance, hands, arms, and all the fabric of members that appears in a corpse, and which I called by the name of body.
Occurrebat practerea me nutriri, incedere, sentire, & cogitare: quas quidem actiones ad animam referebam. It further occurred to me that I was nourished, that I walked, perceived, and thought, and all those actions I referred to the soul;
Sed quid esset haec anima, vel non advertebam, vel exiguum nescio quid imaginabar, instar venti, vel ignis, vel aetheris, quod crassioribus meî partibus esset infusum. but what the soul itself was I either did not stay to consider, or, if I did, I imagined that it was something extremely rare and subtile, like wind, or flame, or ether, spread through my grosser parts.
De corpore verò ne dubitabam quidem, sed distincte me nosse arbitrabar ejus naturam, quam si forte, qualem mente concipiebam, describere tentassem, sic explicuissem: per corpus intelligo illud omne quod aptum est figurâ aliquâ terminari, loco circumscribi, spatium sic replere, ut ex eo aliud omne corpus excludat; tactu, visu, auditu, gustu, vel odoratu percipi, necnon moveri pluribus modis, non quidem a seipso, sed ab alio quopiam a quo tangatur: namque habere vim seipsum movendi, item sentiendi, vel cogitandi, nullo pacto ad naturam corporis pertinere judicabam; quinimo mirabar potius tales facultates in quibusdam corporibus reperiri. As regarded the body, I did not even doubt of its nature, but thought I distinctly knew it, and if I had wished to describe it according to the notions I then entertained, I should have explained myself in this manner: By body I understand all that can be terminated by a certain figure; that can be comprised in a certain place, and so fill a certain space as therefrom to exclude every other body; that can be perceived either by touch, sight, hearing, taste, or smell; that can be moved in different ways, not indeed of itself, but by something foreign to it by which it is touched [and from which it receives the impression]; for the power of self-motion, as likewise that of perceiving and thinking, I held as by no means pertaining to the nature of body; on the contrary, I was somewhat astonished to find such faculties existing in some bodies.
6. Quid autem nunc, ubi suppono deceptorem aliquem potentissimum, &, si fas est dicere, malignum, datâ operâ in omnibus, quantum potuit, me delusisse? 6. But [as to myself, what can I now say that I am], since I suppose there exists an extremely powerful, and, if I may so speak, malignant being, whose whole endeavors are directed toward deceiving me?
Possumne affirmare me habere vel minimum quid ex iis omnibus, quae jam dixi ad naturam corporis pertinere? Can I affirm that I possess any one of all those attributes of which I have lately spoken as belonging to the nature of body?
Attendo, cogito, revolvo, nihil occurrit; After attentively considering them in my own mind, I find none of them that can properly be said to belong to myself.
fatigor eadem frustrà repetere. To recount them were idle and tedious.
Quid verò ex iis quae animae tribuebam? Let us pass, then, to the attributes of the soul.
Nutriri vel incedere? Quandoquidem jam corpus non habeo, haec quoque nihil sunt nisi figmenta. The first mentioned were the powers of nutrition and walking; but, if it be true that I have no body, it is true likewise that I am capable neither of walking nor of being nourished.
Sentire? Nempe etiam hoc non fit sine corpore, & permulta sentire visus sum in somnis quae deinde animadverti me non sensisse. Perception is another attribute of the soul; but perception too is impossible without the body; besides, I have frequently, during sleep, believed that I perceived objects which I afterward observed I did not in reality perceive.
Cogitare? Hîc invenio: cogitatio est; hacc sola a me divelli nequit. Thinking is another attribute of the soul; and here I discover what properly belongs to myself. This alone is inseparable from me.
Ego sum, ego existo; certum est. Quandiu autem? I am – I exist: this is certain; but how often?
Nempe quandiu cogito; nam forte etiam fieri posset, si cessarem ab omni cogitatione, ut illico totus esse desinerem. As often as I think; for perhaps it would even happen, if I should wholly cease to think, that I should at the same time altogether cease to be.
Nihil nunc admitto nisi quod necessario sit verum; I now admit nothing that is not necessarily true.
sum igitur praecise tantùm res cogitans, id est, mens, sive animus, sive intellectus, sive ratio, voces mihi priùs significationis ignotae. I am therefore, precisely speaking, only a thinking thing, that is, a mind (mens sive animus), understanding, or reason, terms whose signification was before unknown to me.
Sum autem res vera, & vere existens; sed qualis res? I am, however, a real thing, and really existent; but what thing?
Dixi, cogitans. The answer was, a thinking thing.
7. Quid praeterea? 7. The question now arises, am I aught besides?
Imaginabor: non sum compages illa membrorum, quae corpus humanum appellatur; non sum etiam tenuis aliquis aër istis membris infusus, non ventus, non ignis, non vapor, non halitus, non quidquid mihi fingo: supposui enim ista nihil esse. I will stimulate my imagination with a view to discover whether I am not still something more than a thinking being. Now it is plain I am not the assemblage of members called the human body; I am not a thin and penetrating air diffused through all these members, or wind, or flame, or vapor, or breath, or any of all the things I can imagine;
Manet positio: nihilominus tamen ego aliquid sum. for I supposed that all these were not, and, without changing the supposition, I find that I still feel assured of my existence.
Fortassis verò contingit, ut haec ipsa, quae suppono nihil esse, quia mihi sunt ignota, tamen in rei veritate non differant ab eo me quem novi? But it is true, perhaps, that those very things which I suppose to be non-existent, because they are unknown to me, are not in truth different from myself whom I know.
Nescio, de hac re jam non disputo; de iis tantùm quae mihi nota sunt, judicium ferre possum. This is a point I cannot determine, and do not now enter into any dispute regarding it. I can only judge of things that are known to me:
Novi me existere; quaero quis sim ego ille quem novi. I am conscious that I exist, and I who know that I exist inquire into what I am.
Certissimum est hujus sic praecise sumpti notitiam non pendere ab iis quae existere nondum novi; non igitur ab iis ullis, quae imaginatione effingo. It is, however, perfectly certain that the knowledge of my existence, thus precisely taken, is not dependent on things, the existence of which is as yet unknown to me: and consequently it is not dependent on any of the things I can feign in imagination.
Atque hoc verbum, effingo, admonet me erroris mei: nam fingerem reverà, si quid me esse imaginarer, quia nihil aliud est imaginari quàm rei corporeae figuram, seu imaginem, contemplari. Moreover, the phrase itself, I frame an image (effingo), reminds me of my error; for I should in truth frame one if I were to imagine myself to be anything, since to imagine is nothing more than to contemplate the figure or image of a corporeal thing;
Jam autem certò scio me esse, simulque fieri posse ut omnes istae imagines, & generaliter quaecunque ad corporis naturam referuntur, nihil sint praeter insomnia. but I already know that I exist, and that it is possible at the same time that all those images, and in general all that relates to the nature of body, are merely dreams [or chimeras].
Quibus animadversis, non minus ineptire videor, dicendo: imaginabor, ut distinctius agnoscam quisnam sim, quàm si dicerem: jam quidem sum experrectus, videoque nonnihil veri, sed quia nondum video satis evidenter, datâ operâ obdormiam, ut hoc ipsum mihi somnia verius evidentiusque repraesentent From this I discover that it is not more reasonable to say, I will excite my imagination that I may know more distinctly what I am, than to express myself as follows: I am now awake, and perceive something real; but because my perception is not sufficiently clear, I will of express purpose go to sleep that my dreams may represent to me the object of my perception with more truth and clearness.
Itaque cognosco nihil eorum quae possum imaginationis ope comprehendere, ad hanc quam de me habeo notitiam pertinere, mentemque ab illis diligentissime esse avocandam, ut suam ipsa naturam quàm distinctissime percipiat. And, therefore, I know that nothing of all that I can embrace in imagination belongs to the knowledge which I have of myself, and that there is need to recall with the utmost care the mind from this mode of thinking, that it may be able to know its own nature with perfect distinctness.
8. Sed quid igitur sum? 8. But what, then, am I?
Res cogitans. A thinking thing, it has been said.
Quid est hoc? But what is a thinking thing?
Nempe dubitans, intelligens, affirmans, negans, volens, nolens, imaginans quoque, & sentiens. It is a thing that doubts, understands, [conceives], affirms, denies, wills, refuses; that imagines also, and perceives.
9. Non pauca sanè haec sunt, si cuncta ad me pertineant. 9. Assuredly it is not little, if all these properties belong to my nature
Sed quidni pertinerent? But why should they not belong to it?
Nonne ego ipse sum qui jam dubito ferè de omnibus, qui nonnihil tamen intelligo, qui hoc unum verum esse affirmo, nego caetera, cupio plura nosse, nolo decipi, multa vel invitus imaginor, multa etiam tanquam a sensibus venientia animadverto? Am I not that very being who now doubts of almost everything; who, for all that, understands and conceives certain things; who affirms one alone as true, and denies the others; who desires to know more of them, and does not wish to be deceived; who imagines many things, sometimes even despite his will; and is likewise percipient of many, as if through the medium of the senses.
Quid est horum, quamvis semper dormiam, quamvis etiam is qui me creavit, quantum in se est, me deludat, quod non aeque verum sit ac me esse? Is there nothing of all this as true as that I am, even although I should be always dreaming, and although he who gave me being employed all his ingenuity to deceive me?
Quid est quod a meâ cogitatione distinguatur? Quid est quod a me ipso separatum dici possit? Is there also any one of these attributes that can be properly distinguished from my thought, or that can be said to be separate from myself?
Nam quod ego sim qui dubitem, qui intelligam, qui velim, tam manifestum est, ut nihil occurrat per quod evidentius explicetur. For it is of itself so evident that it is I who doubt, I who understand, and I who desire, that it is here unnecessary to add anything by way of rendering it more clear.
Sed verò etiam ego idem sum qui imaginor; nam quamvis fortè, ut supposui, nulla prorsus res imaginata vera sit, vis tamen ipsa imaginandi revera existit, & cogitationis meae partem facit. And I am as certainly the same being who imagines; for although it may be (as I before supposed) that nothing I imagine is true, still the power of imagination does not cease really to exist in me and to form part of my thought.
Idem denique ego sum qui sentio, sive qui res corporeas tanquam per sensus animadverto: videlicet jam lucem video, strepitum audio, calorem sentio. In fine, I am the same being who perceives, that is, who apprehends certain objects as by the organs of sense, since, in truth, I see light, hear a noise, and feel heat.
Falsa haec sunt, dormio enim. But it will be said that these presentations are false, and that I am dreaming.
At certe videre videor, audire, calescere. Let it be so. At all events it is certain that I seem to see light, hear a noise, and feel heat;
Hoc falsum esse non potest; hoc est proprie quod in me sentire appellatur; atque hoc praecise sic sumptum nihil aliud est quàm cogitare. this cannot be false, and this is what in me is properly called perceiving (sentire), which is nothing else than thinking.
10. Ex quibus equidem aliquanto melius incipio nosse quisnam sim; sed adhuc tamen videtur, 10. From this I begin to know what I am with somewhat greater clearness and distinctness than heretofore.
nec possum abstinere quin putem, res corporeas, quarum imagines cogitatione formantur, & quas ipsi sensus explorant, multo distinctius agnosci quàm istud nescio quid meî, quod sub imaginationem non venit: quanquam profecto sit mirum, res quas animadverto esse dubias, ignotas, a me alienas, distinctius quàm quod verum est, quod cognitum, quàm denique me ipsum, a me comprehendi. But, nevertheless, it still seems to me, and I cannot help believing, that corporeal things, whose images are formed by thought [which fall under the senses], and are examined by the same, are known with much greater distinctness than that I know not what part of myself which is not imaginable; although, in truth, it may seem strange to say that I know and comprehend with greater distinctness things whose existence appears to me doubtful, that are unknown, and do not belong to me, than others of whose reality I am persuaded, that are known to me, and appertain to my proper nature; in a word, than myself.
Sed video quid sit: gaudet aberrare mens mea, necdum se patitur intra veritatis limites cohiberi. But I see clearly what is the state of the case. My mind is apt to wander, and will not yet submit to be restrained within the limits of truth.
Esto igitur, & adhuc semel laxissimas habe nas ei permittamus, ut, illis paulo post opportune reductis, facilius se regi patiatur. Let us therefore leave the mind to itself once more, and, according to it every kind of liberty [permit it to consider the objects that appear to it from without], in order that, having afterward withdrawn it from these gently and opportunely [and fixed it on the consideration of its being and the properties it finds in itself], it may then be the more easily controlled.
11. Consideremus res illas quae vulgo putantur omnium distinctissime comprehendi: corpora scilicet, quae tangimus, quae videmus; non quidem corpora in communi, generales enim istae perceptiones aliquantò magis confusae esse solent, sed unum in particulari. 11. Let us now accordingly consider the objects that are commonly thought to be [the most easily, and likewise] the most distinctly known, viz, the bodies we touch and see; not, indeed, bodies in general, for these general notions are usually somewhat more confused, but one body in particular.
Sumamus, exempli causâ, hanc ceram: nuperrime ex favis fuit educta; nondum amisit omnem saporem sui mellis; nonnihil retinet odoris florum ex quibus collecta est; ejus color, figura, magnitudo, manifesta sunt; dura est, frigida est, facile tangitur, ac, si articulo ferias, emittet sonum; Take, for example, this piece of wax; it is quite fresh, having been but recently taken from the beehive; it has not yet lost the sweetness of the honey it contained; it still retains somewhat of the odor of the flowers from which it was gathered; its color, figure, size, are apparent (to the sight); it is hard, cold, easily handled; and sounds when struck upon with the finger.
omnia denique illi adsunt quae requiri videntur, ut corpus aliquod possit quàm distinctissime cognosci. In fine, all that contributes to make a body as distinctly known as possible, is found in the one before us.
Sed ecce, dum loquor, igni admovetur: saporis reliquiae purgantur, odor expirat, color mutatur, figura tollitur, crescit magnitudo, fit liquida, fit calida, vix tangi potest, nec jam, si pulses, emittet sonum. But, while I am speaking, let it be placed near the fire – what remained of the taste exhales, the smell evaporates, the color changes, its figure is destroyed, its size increases, it becomes liquid, it grows hot, it can hardly be handled, and, although struck upon, it emits no sound.
Remanetne adhuc eadem cera? Does the same wax still remain after this change?
Remanere fatendum est; nemo negat, nemo aliter putat. It must be admitted that it does remain; no one doubts it, or judges otherwise.
Quid erat igitur in eâ quod tam distincte comprehendebatur? What, then, was it I knew with so much distinctness in the piece of wax?
Certe nihil eorum quae sensibus attingebam; nam quaecunque sub gustum, vel odoratum, vel visum, vel tactum, vel auditum veniebant, mutata jam sunt: remanet cera. Assuredly, it could be nothing of all that I observed by means of the senses, since all the things that fell under taste, smell, sight, touch, and hearing are changed, and yet the same wax remains.
12. Fortassis illud erat quod nunc cogito: nempe ceram ipsam non quidem fuisse istam dulcedinem mellis, nec florum fragrantiam, nec istam albedinem, nec figuram, nec sonum, sed corpus quod mihi apparebat paulo ante modis istis conspicuum, nunc diversis. 12. It was perhaps what I now think, viz, that this wax was neither the sweetness of honey, the pleasant odor of flowers, the whiteness, the figure, nor the sound, but only a body that a little before appeared to me conspicuous under these forms, and which is now perceived under others.
Quid est autem hoc praecise quod sic imaginor? But, to speak precisely, what is it that I imagine when I think of it in this way?
Attenda mus, &, remotis iis quae ad ceram non pertinent, videamus quid supersit: nempe nihil aliud quàm extensum quid, flexibile, mutabile. Let it be attentively considered, and, retrenching all that does not belong to the wax, let us see what remains. There certainly remains nothing, except something extended, flexible, and movable.
Quid verò est hoc flexibile, mutabile? But what is meant by flexible and movable?
An quod imaginor, hanc ceram ex figurâ rotundâ in quadratam, vel ex hac in triangularem verti posse? Is it not that I imagine that the piece of wax, being round, is capable of becoming square, or of passing from a square into a triangular figure?
Nullo modo; nam innumerabilium ejusmodi mutationum capacem eam esse comprehendo, nec possum tamen innumerabiles imaginando percurrere; nec igitur comprehensio hacc ab imaginandi facultate perficitur. Assuredly such is not the case, because I conceive that it admits of an infinity of similar changes; and I am, moreover, unable to compass this infinity by imagination, and consequently this conception which I have of the wax is not the product of the faculty of imagination.
Quid extensum? Nunquid etiam ipsa ejus extensio est ignota? But what now is this extension? Is it not also unknown?
Nam in cerâ liquescente fit major, major in ferventi, majorque rursus, si calor augeatur; nec recte judicarem quid sit cera, nisi putarem hanc etiam plures secundùm extensionem varietates admittere, quàm fuerim unquam imaginando complexus. for it becomes greater when the wax is melted, greater when it is boiled, and greater still when the heat increases; and I should not conceive [clearly and] according to truth, the wax as it is, if I did not suppose that the piece we are considering admitted even of a wider variety of extension than I ever imagined,
Superest igitur ut concedam, me nequidem imaginari quid sit haec cera, sed solâ mente percipere; I must, therefore, admit that I cannot even comprehend by imagination what the piece of wax is, and that it is the mind alone (mens, Lat., entendement, F.) which perceives it.
dico hanc in particulari, de cerâ enim in communi clarius est. I speak of one piece in particular; for as to wax in general, this is still more evident.
Quaenam verò est haec cera, quae non nisi mente percipitur? But what is the piece of wax that can be perceived only by the [understanding or] mind?
Nempe eadem quam video, quam tango, quam imaginor, eadem denique quam ab initio esse arbitrabar. It is certainly the same which I see, touch, imagine; and, in fine, it is the same which, from the beginning, I believed it to be.
Atqui, quod notandum est, ejus perceptio non visio, non tactio, non imaginatio est, nec unquam fuit, quamvis prius ita videretur, sed solius mentis inspectio, quae vel imperfecta esse potest & confusa, ut prius erat, vel clara & distincta, ut nunc est, prout minus vel magis ad illa ex quibus constat attendo. But (and this it is of moment to observe) the perception of it is neither an act of sight, of touch, nor of imagination, and never was either of these, though it might formerly seem so, but is simply an intuition (inspectio) of the mind, which may be imperfect and confused, as it formerly was, or very clear and distinct, as it is at present, according as the attention is more or less directed to the elements which it contains, and of which it is composed.
13. Miror verò interim quàm prona sit mea mens in errores; 13. But, meanwhile, I feel greatly astonished when I observe [the weakness of my mind, and] its proneness to error.
nam quamvis haec apud me tacitus & sine voce considerem, haereo tamen in verbis ipsis, & fere decipior ab ipso usu loquendi. For although, without at all giving expression to what I think, I consider all this in my own mind, words yet occasionally impede my progress, and I am almost led into error by the terms of ordinary language.
Dicimus enim nos videre ceram ipsammet, si adsit, non ex colore vel figurâ eam adesse judicare. We say, for example, that we see the same wax when it is before us, and not that we judge it to be the same from its retaining the same color and figure:
Unde concluderem statim: ceram ergo visione oculi, non solius mentis inspectione, cognosci; nisi jam forte respexissem ex fenestrâ homines in plateâ transeuntes, quos etiam ipsos non minus usitate quàm ceram dico me videre. whence I should forthwith be disposed to conclude that the wax is known by the act of sight, and not by the intuition of the mind alone, were it not for the analogous instance of human beings passing on in the street below, as observed from a window.
Quid autem video praeter pileos & vestes, sub quibus latere possent automata? In this case I do not fail to say that I see the men themselves, just as I say that I see the wax; and yet what do I see from the window beyond hats and cloaks that might cover artificial machines, whose motions might be determined by springs?
Sed judico homines esse. Atque ita id quod putabam me videre oculis, solâ judicandi facultate, quae in mente meâ est, comprehendo. But I judge that there are human beings from these appearances, and thus I comprehend, by the faculty of judgment alone which is in the mind, what I believed I saw with my eyes.
14. Sed pudeat supra vulgus sapere cupientem, ex formis loquendi quas vulgus invenit dubitationem quaesivisse; pergamusque deinceps, attendendo utrùm ego perfectius evidentiusque percipiebam quid esset cera, cùm primùm aspexi, credidique me illam ipso sensu externo, vel saltem sensu communi, ut vocant, id est potentiâ imaginatrice, cognoscere? 14. The man who makes it his aim to rise to knowledge superior to the common, ought to be ashamed to seek occasions of doubting from the vulgar forms of speech: instead, therefore, of doing this, I shall proceed with the matter in hand, and inquire whether I had a clearer and more perfect perception of the piece of wax when I first saw it, and when I thought I knew it by means of the external sense itself, or, at all events, by the common sense (sensus communis), as it is called, that is, by the imaginative faculty;
an verò potiùs nunc, postquam diligentiùs investigavi tum quid ea sit, tum quomodo cognoscatur? or whether I rather apprehend it more clearly at present, after having examined with greater care, both what it is, and in what way it can be known.
Certe hac de re dubitare esset ineptum; nam quid fuit in primâ perceptione distinctum? It would certainly be ridiculous to entertain any doubt on this point. For what, in that first perception, was there distinct?
Quid quod non a quovis animali haberi posse videretur? What did I perceive which any animal might not have perceived?
At verò cùm ceram ab externis formis distinguo, & tanquam vestibus detractis nudam considero, sic illam revera, quamvis adhuc error in judicio meo esse possit, non possum tamen sine humanâ mente percipere. But when I distinguish the wax from its exterior forms, and when, as if I had stripped it of its vestments, I consider it quite naked, it is certain, although some error may still be found in my judgment, that I cannot, nevertheless, thus apprehend it without possessing a human mind.

Commentary

Summary

Paragraph 1

Notice how incongruous this rhetoric is with the conclusion of the first meditation, where Descartes stated that he was going to force himself to adopt a skeptical hypothesis, even though he naturally falls back into common sense beliefs. Here Descartes writes that he has "fallen unexpectedly into a deep whirlpool which tumbles me around so that I can neither stand on the bottom nor swim up to the top." Is that an apt metaphor for artificially forcing oneself to doubt one's everyday beliefs? Descartes seems to have forgotten the end of the previous meditation, and has actually come to believe the evil demon hypothesis, instead of merely supposing it in the quest to see if any propositions are indubitable. Why is there such an emotional valence to his language? The reason is that Descartes is really referring here to the abolishment of the Timean-Aristotelian worldview, rather than to skepticism. What had been previously taken as a firm foundation, the Earth itself, at the center of a beautiful universe of crystal spheres spinning with mathematical order, has been replaced by the atomistic void. There is no bottom nor top, because there is no absolute up or down in the atomistic system. Descartes writes: "Archimedes used to demand just one firm and immovable point in order to shift the entire earth; so I too can hope for great things if I manage to find just one thing, however slight, that is certain and unshakeable." Descartes phrases this search as an epistemological one: he ostensibly searches for one proposition that is not susceptible to the skeptical argument. But it is actually a metaphysical search: he searches for some object in the world that the Church Fathers have not been wrong about, some object which will sustain his religious faith.

Descartes thus establishes a binary value structure, where Forms, Knowledge, and Christianity are goods, and Atomism, Skepticism, Atheism are evils. Of course, he will concede that substantial forms do not exist except in the case of the human soul. But he is anxious to replace the Aristotelian model of knowledge of the forms with knowledge of the underlying conditions of materialistic object, namely space, time, and the laws of nature. (Kant would be following this line of argumentation closely.)

Paragraph 5

This passage may be the reason why commentators have described Descartes's project as an attempt to replace Aristotelian-Aquinian hylomorphism. He describes his prior understanding of himself as a body-soul synthesis. But I think commentators have been too trusting to Descartes here. The Christian understanding of the self has always been more Platonic than Aristotelian. The Christian understanding of the self has been as a magical entity, the soul, that is immaterial and persists after death. Descartes's purported belief in a Stoic conception of the soul as an ether spread throughout the body seems disingenous. Pietro Pomponazzi's book espousing a similar view was burned, and Pomponazzi nearly burned along with it. Adherence to the belief in the immortality of the soul is ispo facto a belief in oneself as something-more-than-corporeal. Insofar as I am a bodily human at the moment, the Christian might say, I am a hylomorphic synthesis of my body and my soul, but after my death, when I am in Purgatory or Heavan, I will be pure soul. Thus Descartes's final position that man is a thinking thing is not as revolutionary as it is often presented. It is rather a retreat from hylomorphism to a more "secure" position, since hylomorphism has become suspect after the Aristotelian-Aquinian cosmology was found to be false.

And even Descartes's description of the body seems rather to anticipate his dualism, rather than to say what his common sense beliefs were. His description of body sounds rather like the Aristotelian prime matter, or of pure space, rather than the everyday world of macroscopic objects.

Paragraph 6

To Descartes's claim that "if I should wholly cease to think, that I should at the same time altogether cease to be," every undergraduate classroom will have one objector who says "but what about a dreamless sleep? I don't cease to exist during a dreamless sleep." The professor usually makes light of this objection, saying that even during your dreamless sleep you are still listening, you can be woken up by an alarm, etc. But the fact is that the professor is forced to make light of this objection because it is so devastating. There is no point in continuing on in the Meditations once you have reached this reductio ad absurdum, so the professor makes light of the objection so that the class can go on. But the truth is that there is no point in continuing on in the Mediations after this point (if we're after the truth)! Descartes's position is refuted by plain evidence, namely that we do persist even during dreamless sleep. This demonstrates that we are really our bodies; they allow for our persistence through dreamless sleep.

Paragraph 7

Descartes here confuses conceivability with possibility. Indeed, it is arguable that whole exercise of radical skepticism was simply a way to support premise (1) below. His argument might be explicated as follows:

  1. I can imagine a world where I exist but in which there are no bodies.
  2. If I can imagine a state of affairs, then that state of affairs is possible.
  3. It is possible that I exist but there are no bodies.
  4. Therefore, I am not a body.

Premise (1) is false, on my view, since we are nothing but bodies. But even if we concede premise (1), premise (2) is also false. It is a delusion of grandeur to suppose that our imagination tracks metaphysical or physical possibility. Why would it? The hidden presupposition for doing so is a presupposition of our rational, semi-divine nature, which is mystically connected to God or the divine Logos. And this presupposition is a veritable cancer at the heart of contemporary Analytic philsophy. Consider David Chalmers's very similar and very influential line of argumentation in The Consious Mind. It could be summarized as:

  1. It is logically possible for a physically identical copy of oneself to exist, but for it to lack conscious experience.
  2. (2) If (1) is true, then facts about conscious experience are not identical to facts about physical constitutions.
  3. (3) Therefore, facts about conscious experience are not identical to facts about physical constitutions.

There is a lot of Analytic jargon here that Descartes did not have at his disposal, such as "logical possibility" and "facts" as entities (indeed, the metaphysically most basic entities, according to Analytic philosophers). And, yes, Chalmers was conceding that conscious facts supervene on physical facts, etc. But once you cut through all the curlicues, the argument is fundamentally the same. I can imagine myself as a non-physical being (or I can imagine a physical copy of myself that is not me), therefore I am a non-physical being. But both argument stem from the same misprision, that our imaginative capcity (which Chalmers calls "logical possibiliy") is connected to the metaphysical structure of the universe. (This fantasy dates back to at least the Ancient Stoics, and Kant would perfect it.) Thus, we are left with the same line of resistance to Chalmers's argument as to Descartes. Premise (1) is false, since concious experience is nothing but a certain electro-chemical reaction in my body. But premise (2) is also false, because the human notion of "logical possibility" has no one-to-one correspondence with the way the universe actually is. Someone like Leibniz would claim that action at a distance is logically impossible, yet it exists.

In addition, Descartes's argument is quasi-question begging, because it presupposes the distinctness of mind and body. He presupposes that, when he is thinking about thoughts, he is not thinking about body. But, if thoughts are nothing but brain states, then this presupposition is false. He is always thinking about bodies, and he cannot conceive of anything that is not a body. There is therefore an asymmetry in his argument, similar to the asymmetry in the dream-skepticism argument. For someone who thinks that mind and body could be distinct, the argument goes forward. For the materialist, the argument is not persuasive. Does the presupposition that mind and body are possibly distinct have any plausibility of its own, which could win over the neutrals? Clearly it does, for it still persuades today and it gave rise to a whole school of Cartesian dualists. The challenge for the Epicurean is to identify why it has prima facie persuasiveness and to attempt to deflate that. But that is the subject of a different essay.

Paragraph 9

It is curious that the most popular resistance to this line of argumentation is not to assert that we are our bodies, and that the unity of our bodies grounds the unity of our selves. Rather, the most hardcore Analytic and Continental philosophers (truly the most dedicated truth-seekers out there, I'm sure) resisted Descartes's arguments by saying "No, you didn't take the skeptical line far enough; the demon could be deceiving you into thinking that you really exist, when in fact you don't. All there really is is a succession of brain states or a clash of psychic forces, or whatever." It might impress the attractive-but-depressed graduate student, but it's complete rubbish.

Paragraph 10

It seems strange that Descartes would oscillate back to a commonsense point of view at this stage in his argument, in asserting that bodies are more distinctly known than the ego, which he had been describing. Of course, this is the true position. Bodies are more distinctly known than the ego, which is an abstract object constructed out of our 2nd-order reflections on our own mental states. It is also problematic for my thesis – that Descartes is trying primarily to resist materialism in the Meditations – that he would present a sort of materialist rebuttal to his argument here, and, particularly, that he would phrase it as something he "cannot help believing." But probably he thinks the success of the famous wax argument in the next passage is so complete that it will redound against this rebuttal completely. And Descartes did not operate on the paradigm that a commonsense view is more likely to be true; quite the reverse.

Is it true that we should believe less strongly in something that is susceptible to doubt than something is not susceptible to doubt? And thus that we should believe more strongly in our own existence than the existence of external bodies, as Descartes suggests? This seems like a good principle to operate under. One false presupposition, however, is that the our own existence is not susceptible to doubt. This would be hinted at in Hobbes's Objection: "Compare 'I am thinking, therefore I am thought' with 'I am walking, therefore I am a walk.'" And of course Daniel Dennett would lake make a whole career out of not just doubting his own existence, but positively denying it and offering arguments against it. But I would actually like to take issue with the principle itself, if we phrase it as "we should believe less strongly in something that is susceptible to skeptical arguments than something is not susceptible to skeptical arguments" Skeptical arguments are strange beasts. They have a sort of social component, which is to raise our standards for what counts as knowledge – as if in self-defense – whenever we hear them. Consider a proposition like "the human mind is weak and true knowledge is unobtainable." Is this susceptible to skeptical arguments? No, because it is the skeptical conclusion. Should we believe in it more strongly than the existence of external objects? We should always keep in our mind the proposition that skeptical arguments are not evidence. And we should regulate our credences according to the evidence available, not according to the lines of argumentation available.

Paragraphs 10 & 11

Descartes's argument here might be explicated as:

  1. Our perceptions of objects change whilst the objects remain the same.
  2. Our intuitions of objects do not change whilst the objects remain the same.
  3. If a mental faculty correctly tracks the persistence of objects through property changes, and another faculty does not, then the second faculty is more primary and fundamental than the first.
  4. Therefore, intuition is more primary and fundamental than perception.
Where does this argument go wrong? It presupposes the existence of a faculty of pure mental intuition. But this faculty does not exist. Rather, we understand that the wax is same wax even though it is melted because (1) it occupies the same location as the unmelted wax and we understand from expericne that things cannot magically change location, and (2) we have had prior experience with things melting. Descartes sharpens his own guillotine here, because an inexperienced toddler would likely not understand that the melted wax is the same object as the unmelted wax, thus showing that this is learned through past sense experiences with similar objects, and not at all some sort of direct mental acquantaince with pure corporeal body. The Epicurean can present an understandable causal process whereby sense experiences produce memories and trained neural pathways that allow the human mind to make correct judgments about the persistence of the wax. Descartes, on the other hand, is forced to resort to a magical interaction between the immaterial mind and the distant body, without recourse to the intermediate causal interactions of sense perception and brain interaction.

It's also worth noticing how unjustified the assertion of the faculty of mental intuition is, on Descartes's stated assumption that he is only believing in things that are indubitable. So far, his ontology only consists of a single thinking thing, since the proposition "I exist" is necessarily true whenever it's asserted by a thinking thing. But now he abruptly leaps to claiming that this thinking thing has the capacity to directly intuit 3-dimensionality and corporeality.

Why does Descartes feel obligated to advance the wax argument? He could have simply relied on the argument-from-conceivability to show that immaterial minds exist. Why does he feel that he needs to undermine the importance of sense perception, or conversesly, bolster the importance of pure intution? It seems plausible that it's because Descartes has realized in advance that perception will cause difficulties for his dualist system. "How to explain the interaction between the mind and the body? Shouldn't we just dispense with the idea of an immaterial mind?"" Descartes thus uses attack as the best form of defence and attempts to argue that the external world is "perceived" by the mind alone, without need for the body. Here we begin to see the retreat into the self and the reification of models (in this case Cartesian geometry) to replace ther real world, which will come to dominate Western philosophy henceforth.

Conclusion

It's worthing digging down to see how and why Descartes's argument goes wrong here. It's striking that his method seems so sound: pare away any propositions that are open to doubt until we reach a solid bottom of an indubitable position and then build up our philosophy from there. And yet the "solid bottom" he reaches is a falsity. How does he take a falsity for an indubitable position. It would be less notable if his conclusion, after many steps of argumentation were false. We could forgive him for that. That would just be the nature of deductive reasoning. But what he takes to be his very axiom is false!

But, as we will see, the flaws in his argument are well known, and were pointed out by his interlocutors in the Objections and Replies. They have thus been known for centuries. Another question arises, then, which is why his argument was, and remains to be, so persuasive. Bertrand Russell seems to just take it for granted that the 1st person starting point is the only given, and that everything must be demonstrated from that basis. David Chalmers produces nothing but more sophisticated versions of Descartes's arguments. What is their intuitive appeal, and where does it come from?
